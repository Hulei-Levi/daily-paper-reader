Abstract 7

Post -translational modifications (PTMs) regulate protein function through 8

dependencies among residue chemistry, sequence context, three -dimensional 9

microenvironments and modification states, yet most predictors model sites 10 

independently and do not connect modification propensity to functional consequences. 11 

Here we present ProtSyntax, a PTM -centered protein language model trained on 4.25 12 

million examples spanning 40 PTM classes and supervised for kinase specificity, PTM 13 

crosstalk and enzyme kinetics. ProtSyntax integrates bidirectional long -range modeling 14 

with geometry -gated attention in a sparse mixture -of -experts architecture and uses 15 

adaptive multi -objective learning to couple residue -level PTM syntax to protein -level 16 

function. Across 40 PTM -site benchmarks, ProtSyntax improved mean MCC and AP 17 

by 12.7% and 10.7%, respectively, relative to the best -performing baselines. It also 18 

distinguished authentic sites from structurally incompatible motif decoys, transferred 19 

to rare PTMs, recovered crosstalk, linked PTM perturbations to enzyme -kinetic 20 

changes and identified disease -associated PTM disruptions. Together, ProtSyntax 21 

provides an interpretable framework for decoding PTM regulation across the proteome. 22

1. Introduction 25 

A central challenge in modern protein science is to understand how a genetically 26 

encoded amino acid sequence is converted into a dynamic regulatory molecule inside 27 

the cell 1. Post -translational modifications (PTMs) provide one of the most powerful 28 

mechanisms for this conversion 2. By adding, removing or interconverting chemical 29 

groups on selected residues, PTMs expand the functional vocabulary of proteins far 30 

beyond sequence alone 3, controlling enzymatic activity, molecular recognition 4,31 

localization, degradation, phase behavior and signal propagation 5. Their importance 32

spans nearly every major domain of biology, including cell signalling, chromatin and 33 

epigenetic regulation, metabolism, immunity, neurobiology, cancer, infection, ageing, 34 

drug discovery and protein engineering 6. Yet PTMs are uniquely difficult to model 35 

because they are neither simple residue labels nor static sequence features 7. Whether a 36 

residue can be modified depends on a hierarchy of constraints, including amino -acid 37 

chemistry, local motif order, enzyme -specific recognition, structural accessibility, long -38 

range domain organization, cellular context and the modification state of neighbouring 39 

or distal residues 8. PTM regulation can therefore be viewed as a biochemical language 40 

of the proteome: residues form the alphabet, motifs and three -dimensional 41 

microenvironments define grammar, and combinatorial modification states encode 42 

context -dependent regulatory meaning 9. However, this language remains incompletely 43 

observed. Experimental PTM maps are sparse, condition -specific, biased toward well -44 

studied modification types and insufficient for many rare or transient regulatory events. 45 

Thus, the key computational problem is not only to predict where PTMs occur, but to 46 

learn the rules by which proteins write, read and reinterpret modification states 10 .47 

Existing computational approaches have significantly advanced PTM -site annotation 11 ,48 

progressing from feature -based classifiers to deep neural networks and protein language 49 

models 12 . MusiteDeep, for example, established a general deep -learning framework 50 

that uses ensemble convolutional and capsule networks to predict and visualize multiple 51 

PTM types from local sequence windows 13 . More recently, PTMGPT2 introduced 52 

prompt -based fine -tuning of GPT -2 to exploit transferable sequence representations for 53 

PTM prediction 14 , whereas AstraPTM2 combined ESM -2 embeddings, AlphaFold2 -54 

derived structural features and a context -aware transformer to predict 39 PTM types 55 

across full -length proteins 15 . Collectively, these models have broadened PTM coverage, 56 

reduced dependence on handcrafted features and improved the representation of 57 

sequence and structural context. Nevertheless, their principal learning objective 58 

remains the assignment of modification labels to individual residues. Even when 59 

multiple PTM types, full -length sequences or structural features are incorporated, 60 

PTMs are generally treated as parallel prediction targets rather than as interacting 61 

elements of a shared regulatory system. This site -centric formulation leaves several 62 

fundamental biological questions unresolved. Local sequence motifs alone cannot 63 

explain why a residue is modified while an identical motif elsewhere remains inert, 64 

because modification competence can depend on solvent accessibility and the spatial 65 

convergence of sequence -distant residues into a permissive three -dimensional 66 

microenvironment 16 . Likewise, treating PTM classes as independent labels does not 67 

explicitly capture the cooperative or antagonistic crosstalk through which one 68 

modification alters the establishment, recognition or functional effect of another 17 .69 

Most critically, predicting the mere occurrence of a PTM provides no mechanistic 70

To address this conceptual gap, we introduce ProtSyntax, a foundation protein large 78 

language model specifically architected to comprehensively decode the syntax and 79 

function of PTMs. Recognizing that language models require robust, unified training 80 

corpora to learn generalized rules, we curated a comprehensive, four -million -scale 81 

PTM dataset spanning 40 modification classes, seamlessly augmented with kinase -82 

specific phosphorylation, modification crosstalk, and enzyme kinetic supervision. 83 

Fundamentally, ProtSyntax is built upon an advanced hybrid attention and sparse 84 

Mixture -of -Experts (MoE) architecture, enabling it to efficiently scale massive model 85 

capacity while maintaining computational tractability. Embedded within this 86 

foundational macro -architecture, the core innovation of ProtSyntax lies in its 87 

specialized multi -scale design, integrating four biologically motivated modules to 88 

capture distinct hierarchical levels of PTM grammar: 1) Bio -RoPE adapts positional 89 

encoding to capture both structural periodicity and residue -dependent physicochemical 90 

modulation, embedding essential motif order and amino acid chemistry directly into the 91 

representation space; 2) Bi -Gated DeltaNet achieves highly efficient bidirectional long -92 

context propagation, filtering out incidental local motifs by seamlessly integrating 93 

global N -terminal and C -terminal evidence around candidate residues; 3) Geometric 94 

Gated Attention (GGA) injects residue -frame -based three -dimensional constraints 95 

directly into the attention mechanism, allowing the model to strictly differentiate 96 

between sequence -compatible residues and genuinely permissive 3D structural 97 

microenvironments; and 4) PACE -Nash, an adaptive multi -objective optimization 98 

strategy, dynamically coordinates PTM classification with uncertainty -aware enzyme 99 

kinetic regression, forcing local residue -level syntax and global protein -level function 100 

to shape a shared latent representation. 101

and 10.67%, respectively. More importantly, its capabilities extended beyond 106

2.1 Establishing ProtSyntax as a PTM -aware protein language model 146 

We first asked whether ProtSyntax learns transferable PTM syntax rather than merely 147 

fitting site -level labels. We define PTM syntax as contextual rules linking residue 148 

chemistry, motif organization, three -dimensional microenvironment and long -range 149 

context to modification compatibility, crosstalk and function. We therefore selected five 150 

complementary tests. Cloze -style recovery assesses contextual reconstruction of 151 

missing PTM types or sites; structure -aware decoys distinguish three -dimensional 152 

permissiveness from linear motif recognition; low -resource transfer measures reuse of 153 

biochemical rules across PTM classes; crosstalk reconstruction evaluates conditional 154 

dependencies between modification events; and PTM -to -function coupling tests 155 

whether local syntax is linked to protein -level functional consequences. Together, these 156 

experiments span residue -level completion, structural discrimination, cross -PTM 157 

transfer, combinatorial regulation and functional semantics, providing a multiscale 158 

evaluation of PTM -aware language learning. All baselines were fine -tuned and 159 

evaluated using matched data and protocols. Finally, owing to space constraints in the 160 

main text, we provide a unified mechanistic explanation in Supplementary Section 4.7 161 

for why ProtSyntax consistently outperformed competing methods across these 162

2.1.1 ProtSyntax recovers residue -level PTM syntax by cloze -style inference 164 

To examine whether ProtSyntax can infer missing modification information from 165 

residue -level context, we formulated PTM prediction as two cloze -style language tasks. 166 

In the PTM -type recovery task 20 , the model was provided with a candidate residue, its 167 

flanking sequence context and available structural information, and was asked to 168 

recover the correct PTM type among 40 modification classes. In the PTM -site recovery 169 

task, the model was given a PTM type and a protein segment containing multiple 170 

chemically compatible residues and was required to rank the experimentally modified 171 

residue above unmodified candidates. These tasks mimic masked -token recovery in 172 

language modeling but replace words with biochemical modification events 21 .173 

ProtSyntax achieved the strongest performance across both evaluation settings (Fig ure 174 

2.A ). In PTM -type recovery, ProtSyntax reached an accuracy of 0.842, outperforming 175 

ESM -C+ MLP ( fine -tuned ), SaProt+ MLP ( fine -tuned ) and AstraPTM2, which achieved 176 

accuracies of 0.741, 0.766 and 0.719, respectively. Ablation of Bio -RoPE reduced the 177 

accuracy to 0.801, indicating that PTM -aware positional encoding contributes to the 178 

recovery of modification -type syntax. In PTM -site recovery, ProtSyntax achieved a 179 

site -ranking AUPRC of 0.812, exceeding ESM -C+MLP, SaProt+MLP and AstraPTM2 180 

by 12.3, 9.6 and 8.2 percentage points, respectively. This improvement was most 181 

pronounced in fragments containing multiple chemically compatible residues, 182 

suggesting that ProtSyntax identifies the contextually favored regulatory residue rather 183 

than simply detecting residue -level permissibility. 184 

To distinguish PTM syntax learning from shallow motif recognition, we introduced two 185 

counterfactual perturbation settings. Motif shuffling disrupted residue order while 186 

approximately preserving amino acid composition, whereas residue -rule violation 187 

replaced the central residue with an amino acid chemically incompatible with the target 188 

PTM (Fig ure 2.B1 –B4 ). Both perturbations induced larger decreases in the true -PTM 189 

logit for ProtSyntax than for ESM -C+ MLP ( fine -tuned ), SaProt+ MLP ( fine -tuned ) or 190 

AstraPTM2. These results indicate that ProtSyntax is sensitive to the joint constraints 191 

among residue identity, motif order and PTM type, supporting its role as a PTM -aware 192 

protein language model rather than a conventional site -level classifier. 193

structure -aware microenvironment decoy test to evaluate whether ProtSyntax uses 197 

structural information in a functionally selective manner. For each experimentally 198

2.1.3 ProtSyntax transfers PTM syntax to low -resource modification types 218 

A PTM language model should reuse biochemical rules learned from abundant PTM 219 

classes to recognize sparsely annotated modification types. We evaluated this property 220 

using a leave -one -PTM -type -out transfer setting. Each low -resource PTM type, 221 

including lactoylation, formylation, butyrylation, N-palmitoylation, C-linked 222 

glycosylation, sulfoxidation, nitration and dephosphorylation, was removed during 223 

pretraining and subsequently evaluated under zero -shot representation and few -shot 224 

adaptation settings with 0, 10 or 50 positive samples. 225 

ProtSyntax exhibited substantially higher sample efficiency than fine -tuned ESM -226 

C+linear probe, SaProt+linear probe and PTMGPT2 (Fig ure 2.D ). In the zero -shot 227 

setting, ProtSyntax achieved a rare -PTM macro -AUPRC of 0.432, compared with 228 

0.286, 0.318 and 0.274 for ESM -C, SaProt and PTMGPT2, respectively. With only 10 229 

positive samples, ProtSyntax improved to 0.611, whereas ESM -C, SaProt and 230 

PTMGPT2 reached 0.421, 0.457 and 0.402, respectively. With 50 positive samples, 231 

ProtSyntax reached a macro -AUPRC of 0.742, approaching the fully supervised regime. 232 

Ablation of PACE -Nash impaired performance under both few -shot settings, 233 

suggesting that physicochemical contrastive regularization and multi -objective 234

PTM regulation is frequently combinatorial: one modification can promote, inhibit or 254 

rewire the probability of another depending on sequence and structural context 22 . To 255 

test whether ProtSyntax captures such higher -order syntax, we performed in silico 256 

perturbation experiments on unseen proteins. Candidate regulatory sites were 257 

substituted with alanine, and the resulting changes in the predicted probabilities of 258 

associated PTM events were quantified to infer cooperative or antagonistic 259 

dependencies ( Figure 2.E -F) .260 

In a protein segment containing coupled phosphorylation and SUMOylation events, 261 

alanine substitution of the phosphorylation site reduced the predicted SUMOylation 262 

probability from 0.87 to 0.62, consistent with a cooperative dependency. Conversely, 263 

perturbing a glycosylation site increased the predicted phosphorylation probability 264 

from 0.69 to 0.91, consistent with an antagonistic relationship. To further assess 265 

whether these perturbation responses generalize beyond individual examples, we 266 

curated 100 experimentally annotated PTM crosstalk sites from the PTMcode v2 267 

dataset and applied the same in silico knockout strategy. ProtSyntax correctly recovered 268 

the expected crosstalk relationship for 91% of these sites, substantially outperforming 269 

DeepPCT ( 73%) and ProXTalk (70%) . Together, these examples show that ProtSyntax 270 

does not treat PTM events as independent labels; instead, it encodes conditional 271 

dependencies among modification events and responds to perturbations in a manner 272 

consistent with combinatorial PTM regulation. 273 

2.1.5 ProtSyntax links local PTM syntax to enzyme functional consequences 274 

We next asked whether the PTM syntax learned by ProtSyntax is linked to protein -level 275 

functional consequences. This analysis exploited the shared encoder used for residue -276 

level PTM prediction and full -length enzyme kinetic regression, which allows local 277 

modification representations to be trained together with global functional readouts 23 .278 

We curated enzymes with experimentally annotated or high -confidence PTM sites and 279 

grouped them according to the spatial relationship between the PTM site and functional 280 

regions, including catalytic neighborhoods, substrate -binding pockets, putative 281 

allosteric regions and distal solvent -exposed surfaces. We then performed in silico 282 

modification -blocking or modification -mimetic substitutions and compared the 283 

resulting changes in ProtSyntax PTM scores with changes in K cat , K m and K i.284 

ProtSyntax -predicted ΔPTM scores were strongly associated with enzyme kinetic 285 

perturbations. In enzymes with available kinetic annotations, ΔPTM scores correlated 286 

with Δlog Kcat with a Spearman’s ρ of 0.628, and with Δlog Km and Δlog Ki with ρ287 

values of 0.547 and 0.512, respectively, outperforming the strongest comparator, 288 

CatPred, which achieved corresponding correlations of 0.501, 0.422 and 0.395. PTM 289

3.Discussion 476 

ProtSyntax reframes PTM modeling from independent site annotation to multiscale 477 

regulatory inference. Here, PTM syntax denotes conditional rules whereby residue 478 

chemistry and motif order define local compatibility, long -range sequence context and 479 

three -dimensional geometry determine structural permissiveness, and interacting 480 

modifications shape functional consequences. By learning these dependencies jointly, 481 

ProtSyntax treats site selection, PTM identity, crosstalk and function as coupled aspects 482 

of a context -dependent regulatory event, thereby bridging proteome -scale PTM 483 

mapping with biological interpretation. 484 

ProtSyntax implements this formulation through Bio -RoPE for PTM -aware motif 485 

organization, Bi -Gated DeltaNet for bidirectional long -range context, Geometric Gated 486 

Attention for residue -frame structural constraints and PACE -Nash for coordinating 487 

PTM learning with uncertainty -aware kinetic supervision. Evidence for transferable 488 

PTM syntax extends beyond benchmark gains: ProtSyntax recovered masked PTM 489 

information, rejected structurally invalid motif decoys, transferred to low -resource 490 

modifications, reconstructed conditional crosstalk and linked predicted PTM 491 

perturbations to kinetic changes. These complementary results argue against simple 492 

motif memorization or task -specific fitting. Applications to disease variants and 493 

condensate regulation further illustrate its capacity to generate hypotheses across 494 

sequence, structure and function. Thus, the principal contribution of ProtSyntax is not 495 

merely broader PTM coverage, but a unified framework for inferring where 496 

modifications occur, the contexts that permit them and their potential functional 497 

consequences. An extended discussion is provided in Supplementary Section 7. 498 

Despite these advances, several limitations remain. ProtSyntax is inherently 499 

constrained by the incompleteness and experimental bias of current PTM annotations, 500 

particularly for rare, transient and condition -specific modifications 30 . Furthermore, 501 

although the geometric branch enables structure -aware reasoning, it still relies on 502 

predicted or static protein conformations and therefore cannot fully represent 503 

conformational ensembles, enzyme –substrate encounter states or highly dynamic 504 

molecular environments 31 . Likewise, the current framework links PTM syntax to 505 

function primarily through enzyme kinetic supervision and in silico perturbation, but 506 

does not yet explicitly model PTM occupancy, temporal signaling dynamics, 507 

proteoform diversity or cell -type -specific regulation. Future work should therefore 508

4.Methods 519 

4.1 PTM -language corpus construction and task formulation 520 

To operationalize PTM language learning, we constructed a multi -task protein corpus 521 

in which each supervision source captures a distinct level of PTM grammar. General 522 

PTM site prediction teaches the model residue -level modification compatibility; 523 

kinase -specific phosphorylation prediction introduces enzyme -recognition specificity; 524 

PTM crosstalk prediction captures conditional dependencies between modification 525 

events; and enzyme kinetic regression links local modification syntax to protein -level 526 

biochemical function. Thus, ProtSyntax was not trained as a collection of independent 527 

predictors, but as a shared sequence –structure model exposed to residue identity, motif 528 

order, three -dimensional microenvironment, PTM co -occurrence and functional 529 

consequence within a unified representation space. 530 

For general PTM site prediction, experimentally supported residue -level PTM 531 

annotations were collected from public protein resources and the literature 34 . Records 532 

were mapped to reference protein sequences, standardized by modification type and 533 

residue coordinate, and deduplicated at the sequence –site level 35 . We removed 534 

annotations with ambiguous modification types, non -unique residue localization, 535 

evidence inferred only from homology transfer or text mining, or sequences that could 536 

not be reliably matched to a reference protein 36 . The resulting PTM -language corpus 537 

contained 348,903 positive site -centered samples and 3,902,925 chemically compatible 538 

negative site -centered samples across 40 PTM classes. Each sample was represented as 539 

a 55 -residue window centered on the candidate residue. Positive samples corresponded 540 

to experimentally validated PTM sites, whereas negative samples were sampled from 541 

residues chemically compatible with the target PTM type but lacking an annotation for 542 

that modification. To reduce homology -driven leakage, protein sequences were 543

4.2 .2 Sequence and structure representation 617 

ProtSyntax integrates protein language representations with explicit residue -level 618 

geometry. For each protein sequence, contextual residue embeddings were generated 619 

offline from ESM -C and SaProt using their official pretrained checkpoints. These 620 

pretrained encoders were used strictly as frozen feature extractors; their parameters 621 

were not updated during ProtSyntax training, and no gradients were propagated back 622 

into ESM -C38 and SaProt 39 . ESM -C provides sequence -derived semantic 623 

representations learned from large -scale protein sequences, whereas SaProt contributes 624 

structure -aware residue representations. The resulting fixed embeddings were projected 625 

into a shared hidden space through trainable projection layers and combined with amino 626 

acid token embeddings before being passed into the ProtSyntax encoder. 627 

For structural modeling, AlphaFold2 -predicted PDB structures were used to derive 628 

residue -level geometric features 40 . For each residue, we computed C α coordinates, 629 

backbone -based local rigid frames and pairwise spatial relationships. These features 630 

were not treated as independent descriptors appended after sequence encoding; instead, 631 

they were used by Geometric Gated Attention to constrain residue –residue interactions 632 

within the encoder. Residues with missing coordinates or unreliable structural 633 

confidence were masked in the geometric branch, allowing the corresponding attention 634 

heads to revert to sequence -based semantic attention. This design enables ProtSyntax 635 

to use three -dimensional information when reliable structures are available while 636 

remaining applicable to low -confidence structures, intrinsically disordered regions and 637 

sequence -only inputs. 638 

4.3 ProtSyntax architecture 639 

ProtSyntax is a PTM -centered protein large language model designed to decode post -640 

translational modification syntax and its functional consequences. Unlike conventional 641 

PTM predictors that treat each candidate residue as an independent classification target, 642 

ProtSyntax formulates PTM regulation as a structured protein language problem. In this 643 

framework, PTM syntax refers to the hierarchical rules by which residue chemistry, 644 

local motif order, long -range sequence context, three -dimensional accessibility and 645 

existing modification states jointly determine whether a residue can be modified, which 646 

PTM type is contextually compatible, how different PTMs condition one another, and 647 

how local modification events are translated into protein -level functional outcomes. 648 

ProtSyntax implements this syntax -to -function formulation through a shared sequence –649 

structure encoder coupled with task -specific heads for general PTM -site prediction, 650

4.4 Hyperparameters and Model Evaluation Metrics 791 

For model evaluation, we used Matthews correlation coefficient (MCC), area under the 792 

receiver operating characteristic curve (AUC) and average precision (AP) for 793 

classification tasks, and the coefficient of determination (R 2) and mean absolute error 794 

(MAE) for regression tasks. ProtSyntax was trained on a cluster of 2 4 NVIDIA H100 795 

GPUs and can be deployed for inference on a H100 /A100 /5090 /4090 GPU server. 796 

In ProtSyntax, each sparse MoE layer comprises 16 experts and uses Top -2 routing, 797 

such that only a small subset of experts is activated for each token. The model contains 798 

approximately 4 billion (4B) trainable parameters, with approximately 1.0 billion (A1B) 799 

active parameters per token, thereby balancing large model capacity with efficient 800 

sparse computation. In brief, ProtSyntax used a 33 -layer hybrid backbone with a 3:1 801 

interleaving ratio between Bi -Gated DeltaNet and Geometric Gated Attention blocks 802 

and a hidden dimension of 1,280. Key settings included 20% structural -periodic and 803 

30% physicochemical channels in Bio -RoPE, 16 heads in Bi -Gated DeltaNet, four 804 

geometric probes in Geometric Gated Attention and a contrastive temperature of 0.7 in 805 

PACE -Nash optimization. The model was optimized with AdamW using a peak 806 

learning rate of 4e -4, 5,000 warm -up steps and a task -dependent batch size of 256 or 807 

512. Detailed information regarding the hyperparameters can be found in Section 5 of 808 

the Supplementary Materials. To facilitate broad accessibility, we developed 809 

ProtSyntax Lab, a web -based platform that enables biologists and bioinformaticians to 810 

perform PTM -related prediction tasks through an intuitive online interface. 811
