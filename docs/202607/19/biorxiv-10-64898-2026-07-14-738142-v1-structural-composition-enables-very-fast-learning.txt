ABSTRACT 

There is a gap between neuroscientific theories of learning and the speed of learn-1

ing observed in many experiments. Since the Cognitive Revolution of the 1950s, 2

compositionality has played a central role in efforts to bridge this gap. Roughly, a 3

compositional system is one where distinct modules are combined according to a set 4

of rules in order to accomplish complex tasks. Recently, significant progress has been 5

made in understanding the emergence of modules in both biological and artificial 6

neural systems. How, and under what conditions, the rules of module recombination 7

are represented in these systems remains an open question. Here we present a neural 8

model that can leverage these rules to dramatically speed up learning. We first show 9

that when faced with multiple tasks which share subcomponents, models learn a 10 

low-dimensional representation that captures how subcomponents are reused across 11 

the task set. These low-dimensional spaces encode the structure that governs how 12 

modules should be recombined. Restricting learning to these subspaces greatly 13 

reduces the amount of experience needed to acquire a novel task, even when learning 14 

from reinforcement on single trials. In some cases, we can leverage the geometric 15 

regularities of these representations to reduce learning to a form of hypothesis testing 16 

over a small set of discrete points. Finally, we use this theory to model both behav-17 

ioral and neural data from non-human primates performing a compositional task, and 18 

show that key features in this data are consistent with a model in which exploration 19 

during learning is restricted to these low-dimensional spaces. Overall, this work 20 

shows that the advantages of modularity in neural systems can be greatly improved 21 

upon when models represent the structure of module reuse. Both these features 22 

working in tandem lead to learning on timescales similar to biological intelligences, 23 

and hence provide a model for how such fast, adaptable behavior can emerge from 24 

systems of neurons. 25

Introduction 26 

One of the oldest and most successful ideas in neuroscience is that learning occurs because of slow 27 

changes in synaptic weights [ 1–4 ]. Generally, changing weights to adapt to new circumstances 28 

predicts a gradual acquisition of skills. By contrast, learning observed in many experiments can 29 

occur in a relatively small number of trials or involve sudden insights [ 5 , 6 ]. How to bridge the divide 30 

between slow weight updates and the rapid adaptability of biological intelligence is a core problem 31 

of brain and cognitive science [ 7–9 ]. In the middle of the 20th century, computational cognitive 32 

science proposed a solution: compositionality. A compositional system is one where distinct 33 

modules are combined according to a set of rules in order to accomplish a complex task. Originally, 34 

a framework for understanding the expressivity of natural language [ 10], compositionality soon 35 

spread beyond language to become a more general principle used to explain cognitive phenomena 36 

in domains as diverse as navigation [ 11, 12 ], vision [ 13 –15], motor planning [ 16 ], and reasoning 37 

[17, 18]. 38 

For neuroscientific theories of learning, the appeal of compositionality left open the question of 39 

how such a scheme might be implemented in neural systems. Early neural network models appeared 40 

incapable of implementing composition, sparking debate over whether neural compositionality was 41 

possible in principle [ 19 , 20 ]. However, converging evidence from advances in neural network 42 

modeling and recordings taken during increasingly sophisticated animal behaviors suggests that 43 

compositional computations can indeed emerge in networks of neurons [ 21 –29 ]. In a prominent 44 

line of work studying recurrent neural networks (RNNs) performing a variety of cognitive tasks, the 45 

authors found that networks reused modules across tasks with similar demands (e.g. they reuse the 46 

same ring attractor across tasks that required memorizing a circular variable) [ 21 ]. Importantly, after 47 

the network developed these modules during pre-training, they could be recombined in new ways 48 

to perform novel tasks without changing the recurrent weights. Instead, networks simply needed 49 

to learn a new input which configured the RNN to recruit proper recombination of modules for 50 

the novel task. We call this input, which is typically a high-dimensional vector, a task embedding 51 

vector .52 

The central claim of this work is that if subjects take full advantage of regularities in how 53 

modules are used across the task set, learning can be extremely fast – often requiring only single 54 

digit number of trials. This is possible because, in compositional settings, there are a small number 55 

of dimensions in the space of task embeddings which fully describe how modules are reused, and 56 

those dimensions effectively control the entire system. Restricting learning to these subspaces 57 

results in very fast acquisition of new tasks. In effect, this is a neural implementation of the rules of 58 

module recombination – combinations that are valid according to the experience of pretraining all 59 

reside within these subspaces. 60 

In what follows, we first show empirically that task representations in multitasking RNNs encode 61 

the compositional structure needed to make rapid learning possible in principle. To understand the 62 

emergence of this structure, we analyze a feedforward network trained on analogous compositional 63 

task sets. In this setting, we precisely characterize how the organization of the task set relates to 64 

structure in learned task embeddings, and describe the conditions required for this correspondence 65 

to hold. We conjecture that the mechanism we find in the feedforward network applies to recurrent 66 

networks as well, and validate this across many task sets learned by RNNs. We then show how 67 

systems can use this structure in task embeddings to achieve rapid learning. Finally, we demonstrate 68 

that features of behavioral and neural data recorded from non-human primates performing a 69 

compositional task are consistent with learning that is guided by this structure. 70 

Crucially, these results hold for systems without architectural choices designed to capture 71 

compositional structure. Instead, this structure emerges naturally from performing tasks with 72 

overlapping demands. At a high level, we see improvement in the speed of learning because our 73 

system doesn’t just recombine modularized computations. Rather, by learning in the relevant 74

Results 79 

Modularity and task set structure in multitasking recurrent networks as a path to modeling 80 

rapid learning 81 

We begin with a concrete example. Consider a set of decision-making tasks that consist of multiple 82 

interchangeable parts, chosen to closely resemble those used in the experimental literature [ 29 –83 

32 ]. In these tasks, participants receive sensory input in two input modalities (Mod1 and Mod2) 84 

representing, for example, audition and vision. On every trial the input, which appears in both 85 

modalities, consists of a strong and a weak signal, placed at random points on a circle (Fig. 1a, left 86 

panel). The trial unfolds over time, and includes a fixation period, followed by stimulus presentation, 87 

and then a response period. The demands of each task are defined by three binary axes. The first 88 

determines the input modality to attend to (Mod1 versus Mod2; e.g., pay attention to vision and 89 

ignore audition). The second determines whether to respond to the strongest or the weakest stimuli 90 

presented in that modality (Strong vs. Weak). The third determines the proper method of response 91 

(Resp1 vs. Resp2; e.g., saccade vs. joystick). A schematic of the stimulus and response structure 92 

for example ‘Mod1-Strong-Resp1’ and ‘Mod2-Weak-Resp2’ trials are shown in Fig. 1a (the time 93 

dimension of the task in this figure is omitted for easy visualization; see Methods for full details). 94 

In total 8 tasks can be built out of the 6 task components (Mod1 vs. Mod2; Strong vs. Weak; Resp1 95 

vs. Resp2). Crucially, the sensory inputs by themselves don’t allow participants to infer the task 96 

demands. Therefore, a task cue is provided. In simulations these are encoded as one-hot vectors. 97 

Although the 8 one-hot vectors (which are by definition orthogonal) cannot encode the three 98 

binary axes that organize the task set, it is nonetheless possible to learn the underlying organization 99 

in model weights. Indeed, the reuse of subcomponents according to a latent structure is typical 100 

of learning in these compositional settings across species [ 29 , 33 , 34 ]. Humans in particular 101 

have shown a remarkable aptitude for inferring underlying task rules efficiently, often in only a 102 

handful of trials [ 35, 36 ]. Given some experience with a subset of the tasks described above, we’d 103 

expect humans to learn a held out task in very few trials [ 37 –39 ]. The primary goal of this paper 104 

is to describe the kind of neural architecture and learning algorithm that could account for the 105 

incredible speed with which biological organisms acquire tasks in such settings. We start by showing 106 

empirically that RNNs trained on the above task develop modular structure and some representation 107 

of how modules are reused across tasks. We then focus on the simpler case of bi-linear feedforward 108 

networks where we can characterize this structure precisely and outline conditions where it is 109 

guaranteed to emerge. Empirically, we then show across a variety of different kinds of task sets with 110 

different compositional organizations that the same predicted structure emerges in RNNs. These 111 

representations allow the systems to vastly constrain learning, resulting in very rapid acquisitions 112 

of novel tasks. We then turn to neural data recorded during compositional tasks and show that 113 

important features of this data are compatible with constraining learning in this manner. 114

Examination of the structure of RNN computations suggests an alternative way of choosing 148 

task embeddings that recruit the proper set of modules. Previous studies have noted that directly 149 

prior to stimulus onset the recurrent states of the RNN represent relevant dimensions of the task 150 

set [ 22 , 25]. A visualization of this is shown in Fig. 1d, where the recurrent activity is organized 151

3-dimensional parallelogram geometry where each task resides at one of the vertices of this shape. 153 

It’s worth noting that representations which factorize task components along consistent axes in 154

In the rest of the paper we’ll describe how such fast learning is possible, beginning with an 165 

analysis the structure of task embedding weights in compositional settings. In general, it is difficult 166 

to get an intimate mathematical understanding of nonlinear RNNs. Instead of analyzing RNNs 167 

directly, we hypothesize that the structure observed in Wemb (Fig. 1e) is primarily driven by structure 168 

in the task set, rather than recurrent dynamics. Under this hypothesis, our aim is to gain insight 169 

into the behavior of RNNs by studying similarly organized tasks in simple, analytically tractable, 170 

settings. In the following section we consider a bi-linear system with compositional structure that is 171 

analogous to the example decision-making task set considered above, and use this to demonstrate 172 

how the compositional structure of the task set is encoded in learned weights more generally. In 173 

later sections, we’ll leverage the insights gained about the structure of learned weights to build 174 

algorithms that use the structure of embedding weights to learn new tasks very rapidly. 175

Rapid Learning Using Compositional Structure and Reinforcement 346 

The embedding weights, fWemb , encode the compositional structure of the training set (given small 347 

weight initialization). In particular, we found empirically that fWemb will be low-dimensional, and 348 

the structure of the task set will be encoded in the geometry of eUemb . The key implication of these 349 

facts, and the core thesis of this work, is that learning for a novel but compositionally related task 350 

can proceed in a subspace of activity which is significantly smaller than the full task embedding 351 

space. In this section we show that learning in this constrained space will result in very rapid 352 

acquisition of new tasks. 353 

Let us return to our DM task set with three binary components. Suppose we have a model trained 354

for a correct response. Importantly, the system also does not know that the target task is novel and 358 

so has no reason to exclude training tasks from exploration. The most unconstrained method of 359

Capturing neural signatures of compositional task discovery 466 

In the previous sections we showed how weights in neural networks encoded the structure of module 467 

reuse across a task set, and how leveraging that structure allows for very rapid acquisition of novel 468 

tasks. At a basic level our models describe how neural activity should evolve if agents are using the 469 

compositional structure of the task set to guide learning. However, the speed of learning exhibited 470 

by structural composition opens the possibility of fitting these neural models to the behavior of 471 

animals engaged in compositional tasks, where adaptation to task demands can be very rapid [ 29]. 472 

We can then compare the evolution of activity in our models to data. As we will see, constraining 473 

learning with compositional structure makes specific predictions about how modules are re-engaged 474 

as learning occurs, which we find is a good match to trends observed in data. In this section we 475 

first describe the experiment we intend to model, and outline two alternative models which we fit 476 

to behavior alongside structural composition. From there, we show how learning via structural 477 

composition is the only model which can capture the important features of neural evolution during 478 

the acquisition of task demands. 479 

In a recent experiment testing animals’ compositional abilities, Tafazoli et. al. [ 29 ] trained two 480 

monkeys on a set of decision making tasks defined by 1) the relevant feature of the stimulus and 481 

2) the relevant axis of responses. On the feature dimension, the monkeys had to perform a 2-way 482 

categorization based on either the shape of the stimulus (more ‘Tee’-like or more ‘Bunny’-like) or 483 

the color of the stimulus (more red or more green). The animals then had to report their answer to 484 

the decision-making task using the proper response axis. For response axis 1 trials, animals had to 485 

answer using top-left and bottom-right screen touches; for response axis 2 trials, they had to respond 486 

with top-right and bottom-left screen touches. Animals were pre-trained on the shape-axis1 (S1), 487 

color-axis1 (C1), and color-axis2 tasks (C2) only (Fig. 6a). The decision boundaries for both shape 488 

and color features are shown in Fig. 6b. After pre-training, the animals achieved high performance 489 

in each task individually. The authors then recorded neural activity across multiple brain regions 490 

while the animals transitioned through a sequence of blocks, where trials in each block are from a 491 

single underlying task type (Fig. 6b, bottom). Block transitions were explicitly cued, but the cues 492 

provided no information about the identity of the upcoming task. In this setting, the main task 493 

of the animals is to infer the current task demands using reward feedback alone. This paradigm 494 

allowed researchers to track neural dynamics during “task discovery”; i.e., inference of current task 495 

identity. Though contextual inference has been extensively studied in previous work [ 30 , 43 –45 ], 496 

this experiment is the first to record from non-human primates in a task set where contexts are 497 

made up of distinct compositional components (stimulus feature and response axis). Although in 498 

modeling this experiment we shift focus from learning novel tasks to discovering previously learned 499 

tasks, the design allows us to test our theory of compositional learning (as opposed to many other 500 

contextual inference tasks which lack compositional structure). The basic proposal of our model 501

of the current response axis. The inset of Fig. 6c shows how often animals responded using the 512 

incorrect axis as the block progressed. The number of error drops dramatically within the first few 513 

trials. One important aspect of the experimental design is the sequence of blocks. In particular, a 514 

C2 block is always followed by an S1 or C1 block, and S1 and C1 blocks always transition to C2 515 

blocks. There is therefore, in principle, enough information for animals to do very well on the first 516 

trial of C2 block, and that there is less uncertainty in these blocks because animals don’t have to 517 

adjudicate between shape and color as the relevant feature (this likely accounts for differences in 518 

ceiling performance between block types). Animals sometimes take advantage of block sequencing 519 

information. We include parameters in our models that capture these sequence effects though we 520 

don’t attempt to model sequence learning in a mechanistic way, and instead focus on the dynamics 521 

of task discovery within individual blocks (see Methods). For possible mechanisms capturing this 522 

block sequencing see [46]. 523 

Here we fit our reinforcement learning models to the task discovery trajectories of animals 524 

[5 , 47 ]. We pre-train a multitasking RNN so that it is proficient on all tasks individually (mirroring 525 

animal pre-training) and use these networks to model animals’ task discovery trajectories. To 526 

model animal behavior we pre-train models on the same three tasks (S1, C2, C1; training on C1, 527 

C2, S1, and S2 yields a square geometry in task embedding vectors where axes are defined by 528 

the feature dimension and the response axis dimension, Supplementary Fig. 1). Following the 529 

kinds of learning discussed in previous sections, we assume that the RNN recurrent weights which 530 

implement modular computations are frozen, and instead we model task discovery as primarily a 531 

result of changes in embedding activity which serves as external input to the RNN itself. 532 

Given that the animals have observed all tasks during pre-training, the most straightforward way 533 

to model task discovery is as an inference process, where the animal uses reward to update its belief 534 

that a given task is the true underlying task in that block. At each trial a task embedding is sampled 535 

from this distribution (we call this the Inference Model, for full details see Methods). An example 536 

embedding trajectory in a C1 block for a fitted Inference Model is shown in Fig. 6d. In this model, 537 

because the space of possible embeddings is very small (three points acquired during pre-training) 538 

learning can be very fast. However, it is fundamentally different than the compositional learning we 539 

have been discussing because it does not learn according to the task relevant dimensions that emerge 540 

in embedding weights, and instead simply updates a distribution over the known set of tasks. 541 

Alternatively, animals might discover task demands by updating a the full high-dimensional task 542 

embedding vector. This is exactly the modular learning consider in previous sections. Figure 6e 543 

depicts a trajectory of embedding activity in the same block as Fig. 6d for a fitted Modular model. 544 

Here activity freely evolves in high-dimensional space, meaning some learning occurs along the 545 

relevant dimensions, but also can occur in orthogonal and irrelevant directions. 546

composition (because in this case we don’t impose a strong prior on exploration but still parameterize 548 

the policy using a beta distribution, we drop the strong/weak distinction and simply refer to this 549 

model as structural composition; see Methods for details). In this case we plot the evolution of 550 

low-dimensional control vector c which gets mapped back into the full embedding space. Again, in 551

Discussion 604 

The core claim of this work is that a compositional system which leverages the structure of module 605 

reuse will be able to acquire unseen tasks very rapidly. Our analysis of composition in bi-linear 606 

networks showed that this structure necessarily emerges in learned weights given small weight 607 

initialization. We then conjectured that more complicated non-linear recurrent systems behave in the 608 

same way, and validated this empirically over a variety of task types and compositional structures. 609 

Using this structure allowed us to constrain learning, resulting in very rapid task acquisition. Finally, 610 

we offer initial evidence that monkeys are using the structure of module usage to guide learning 611 

(or in this case task discovery). These results capture a long held intuition taken from classic 612 

cognitive science: that much of learning involves recombining pre-existing knowledge in novel 613 

ways, rather than starting from scratch at every new problem. The key contribution of this work 614 

is to show that under very mild conditions exactly this kind of learning is very easy to do in a 615 

system of neurons, precisely because they neurons will encode a simple representation of which 616 

recombinations are possible. These representations then can then efficiently guide updates in neural 617 

activity that eventually select the proper combination of modules for the task at hand. 618 

In this work we have illustrated the learning mechanisms in systems with knowledge of only 619 

a handful of tasks. In real world settings, we likely enter any situation with a vast repertoire of 620 

modularized skills available to apply. On the one hand, this means that we very rarely have to learn 621 

new sub-skills from scratch; on the other, the number of possible recombinations might be very 622 

large. That said, there is also a vast amount of sensory, social, and linguistic information which 623 

can inform exactly what subset of modules is likely to be relevant [ 37, 48 ]. This leads to a very 624 

general view of how learning proceeds: upon entering a novel situation, executive systems integrate 625 

information about current goals and context to narrow the set of potentially useful modules, and 626 

compositional learning occurs in this narrowed space, leading to adaptive behavior. In a world built 627 

by and for humans inferring context is often automatic (from e.g. built environment, linguistic 628 

and social cues etc.). By contrast, the need to shape animal behavior in experimental settings 629 

can be interpreted as teaching the animal which modules are relevant for the current task [ 49– 52 ]. 630 

Animals still come into the lab with a vast range of modular skills, but little context for how to 631 

apply them in the unnatural world of the laboratory. Shaping narrows the relevant set of skills 632 

to something low-dimensional (corresponding to low-rank task embeddings in our model) which 633 

allows fast and flexible performance in the experiment. Biologically, this process could be achieved 634 

by selectively inhibiting irrelevant dimensions, a mechanism likely localized in frontal regions such 635 

as the lateral prefrontal cortex (lPFC). Concurrently, reward-encoding areas could drive targeted 636 

weight adjustments aligned with the underlying compositional structure. These synaptic changes 637 

would subsequently modulate frontal activity to recruit the appropriate modules. Whatever the case, 638 

the learning advantages afforded by structural composition make it appealing from an evolutionary 639 

standpoint, and the neural evidence points towards the fact that biological brains implement this 640 

strategy. 641 

We’d again like to emphasize that the structure in weights emerges naturally from training on 642 

related tasks, and this requires very few architectural specifications (mainly a separate population of 643 

embedding weights and the right initialization, or possibly the right regularization scheme). Once 644 

the system has reduced control to a low-dimensional space, then learning can be fast. The specific 645 

algorithms proposed in this work are examples of how the system can constrain learning to these 646 

spaces with minimal additional computations, all of which are self-supervised. There is likely 647 

a family of implementations that take take advantage of this structure, but they will all learning 648 

quickly by constraining learning using this structure. Therefore, the central prediction of this model 649

Moreover, because our models are fundamentally neural models of computation, we were able to 659

model, doing inference over an experimenter defined variable like the relevant feature (color vs. 664 

shape) can be directly translated as moving activity along the corresponding dimension in embedding 665

compositionally related tasks. 667 

From a broader perspective, one can conceive of weak and strong structural learning as residing 668 

in a larger hierarchy of learning types that trade off restrictions on what kinds of target tasks 669 

can be learned with the amount of experience required to learn them. At one end, networks and 670 

animals can achieve strong 0-shot performance given instructions for the task, including in the 671 

form of natural language [ 22 , 53 ]. Here no experience is required, but the only task acquired in 672 

this manner is the one being instructed. Strong structural composition can learn tasks that are 673 

within the inclusion/exclusion structure of the pre-training task set (as noted before, this setting 674 

applies to a vast range of experimental and real world settings [ 29, 30, 53 – 58 ]), and only require a 675 

handful of trials to achieve strong performance. Weak structural composition can learn tasks where 676 

modular components are combined or interpolated in the same low-dimensional subspace, and can 677 

learn efficiently with many dozens of trials. Modular composition can learn a target task that is 678 

made up of modules acquired during pre-training combined in any arbitrary manner, but learning 679 

may take many hundreds of trials [ 21 , 59 ]. Finally, more traditional learning schemes that make 680 

updates to all of the weights of the system can learn any input-output mapping. But this can take 681 

thousands of trials, especially when learning from reward feedback, and can cause previous tasks to 682 

be forgotten. There is a rich literature of behavioral modeling that examines fast structural learning 683 

through inference vs. incremental learning via synaptic weight updates [ 46 , 60– 63 ]. One of the key 684 

insights of this work is that structure is not a binary. There are gradations of structure that systems 685 

can take advantage of in order to learn. The tradeoffs of instructed, strong, weak and modular 686 

composition followed by traditional weight updates neatly illustrate a widely held intuition: to learn 687 

quickly the system must making increasingly strong assumptions about how tasks are organized 688 

[ 49 ]. How biological agents gain the skills and experience required to form modules remains an 689 

open question. Theoretical work suggests that biological constraints may lead to regularization that 690 

has similar effect as small weight initializations [ 64 ]. Animal may also acquire this knowledge over 691 

many different timescales: evolutionary [ 49 , 65 ], learning over a life time [ 66 ], or shaping in the 692 

laboratory [50–52]. 693 

Past theoretical work has studied systems similar to the linear compositional networks we used to 694 

derive our analytical results [ 26, 67 ]. In particular [ 23 ] investigated a nonlinear version of the same 695 

basic student-teacher set up and showed that learned module weights in the student must be a linear 696 

combination of module weights in the teacher. While that work mainly focused on the emergence 697 

of modules, our analysis demonstrates how and when the structure of module usage is encoded 698 

in weights at the end of learning. Other recent theoretical work has examined how parallelogram 699

Since some of the first work on neural compositionality, these abstract, parallelogram geometries 707 

have played a central role. Such organization emerges in both multitasking recurrent and feedforward 708 

network models [ 22 , 25, 69 , 70]. In neural recordings, one of the most common proxy measures 709 

of this geometry is the cross-condition decoding used in Fig. 7. High cross decoding accuracy has 710 

been observed in animals and humans performing tasks with overlapping demands [ 33 , 34 , 40, 71]. 711 

Intriguingly, [ 34] observed that if neural representations did form a consistent geometry then 712 

humans quickly switched responses after a change in task demands. In this work we offer a precise 713 

mathematical description of what geometry should emerge given the organization of the task set. 714 

This allows us to predict cross-conditional decoding for any compositional task set organization 715 

prior to training a model. Given the rise of multitasking paradigms and the prevalence of cross-716 

conditional decoding in neural data analysis, such predictions could prove valuable in designing 717 

experiments and explaining their results. 718 

While the importance of abstract geometries for flexible cognition and learning has been recog-719 

nized since these patterns were first identified [ 33], our work provides the first mechanistic model 720 

describing how rapid adaptation depends on this structure. This was possible because our analyzes 721 

showed exactly how structure in the weights matches structure in the task set. This in turn revealed 722 

the small number of directions in weight space that effectively control the entire system. Without the 723 

formal expressions in Eqs. 8a–9b, the presence of representational geometry in population activity 724 

remains highly suggestive, but offers no clear mechanism for updating activity in a way that adheres 725 

to this underlying organization. Finally, our work also shows that this abstract parallelogram geom-726 

etry is an important special case of a much more general framework for predicting the geometry 727 

of representations in non-binary settings, as demonstrated in Fig. 3f-g. Indeed, this framework 728 

may explain work which showed motor learning is significantly faster for tasks parameterized by 729 

continuous variables on a low-dimensional manifold than for tasks with independent parameters 730 

[72]. 731 

The requirement that compositional systems possess a set of rules that govern module recombi-732 

nation has elsewhere been referred to as the syntactic dimension of composition, in rough analogy 733 

with the rules that specify valid linguistic utterances. Structural composition as defined here is but 734 

one relatively simple scheme for constraining module recombination in neural systems. Future work 735 

can build on the understanding we’ve developed in this setting to probe the neural implementation 736 

of syntactic composition in increasingly complicated settings, including sequences, predictive tasks, 737 

navigation, and eventually natural language [28, 73–75]. 738 

Though there are many open questions in this direction, overall we believe this work emphasizes 739 

the fundamental importance of the syntactic aspect of composition. Having modularized skills is 740 

certainly advantageous. But when the system has both modularized skills and a representation of 741 

how those modules can be recombined, then learning can become very fast. Specifying how exactly 742 

these rules are represented by neural systems also leads to a more unified picture of experimental 743 

and simulation results: observed geometries fall directly out of the structure of the task set, and 744 

learning is effected by this geometry because the geometry itself constrains how the system will 745 

learn. Our hope is that examining how neurons represent syntax will lead to an even more coherent 746 

view of compositional mechanisms in increasingly sophisticated task domains, and eventually help 747 

us understand the incredible fluidity of our own cognitive abilities. 748

RNN Modularity Analysis 1146 

To illustrate the modularity of computations in multitasking RNNs (Fig. 1c), we plot the time 1147 

averaged, normalized variance of each unit across tasks [ 25 ]. In Fig. 1c the network was trained 1148 

on the 3-D Decision-Making task set with Mod1-Strong-Resp1 held out of training. Then, for 1149 

each task, we compute the variance across time for each unit using 100 trials, and for each unit 1150 

we normalize by the maximum variance over tasks. For the held out task we cue the network with 1151 

embedding activity extracted from the missing vertex of the underlying parallelogram structure 1152 

(the blue x in Fig. 1e; see Compositional Learning from Reinforcement below). Subsequently, we 1153 

perform k-means clustering by treating the normed variance of each unit as a feature vector over 1154 

tasks. We test values of k between 2 and 30, and we choose the cluster whose k has the maximum 1155 

silhouette score. 1156 

Note that while previous studies performed this analysis for individual trial periods (e.g. clustered 1157 

activity only during the stimulus period), this was primarily important for identifying clusters of 1158 

units in networks trained on tasks with very different timing structures (e.g. networks trained on 1159 

tasks with both a single stimulus, and with two stimuli interspersed with a delay) [ 21 ]. In our 1160 

networks, the structure of timing within a task set is consistent, so analyzing per trial period is not 1161 

necessary to identify the important modular structure in our networks. 1162

Decoding Analyses 1411 

The decoding analyses follow the methods of Tafazoli et al. [ 29 ]. In general, the goal is to track 1412 

the re-engagement of different computational subcomponents by training decoders to map from 1413 

RNN hidden state to some variable (e.g. stimulus color category) in late trials of one block type 1414 

(e.g. C2) where performance is stable. Then we test the decoder on early trials of alternative blocks 1415 

(e.g. C1) to determine whether representations are re-used, and, importantly, if there is a trend in 1416 

cross-decoding performance as the models re-engage computations as the block progresses. To 1417 

balance training distributions for decoder we separate trials by stimulus congruency, which describes 1418 

whether stimulus shape and color features map to the same or different categories. There are four 1419 

stimulus congruency classes in total: 1420 

{(cat color = 0 , cat shape = 0) ,

(cat color = 0 , cat shape = 1) ,

(cat color = 1 , cat shape = 0) ,

(cat color = 1 , cat shape = 1) }

We trained logistic regression decoders for each within-trial time step on stimulus color category, 1421 

stimulus shape category, and response axis direction. Again, following the approach in [ 29], for 1422 

each decoding target we first pool eligible trials into training and testing distributions. We then 1423 

train and test decoders on a small sample from these distributions, repeat this over many different 1424 

decoder iterations, and average across decoders to obtain overall cross decoding performance. For 1425 

each decoding target we trained 50 individual decoder for each of the 5 randomly initialized RNN 1426 

models, resulting in 250 total decoders per trial time step. 1427

Color Decoder Color decoders were trained to decode the color category of the stimulus from RNN 1428 

hidden states. Only the final correct 75 trials of the C2 block were eligible for inclusion in the 1429 

training set. For each decoder, we drew four trials from each of the congruency classes. The test 1430 

distribution was built by taking sliding windows (window size of 40 trials with a slide of 5 trials) 1431 

from the first 110 trials of C1 blocks. For each window, 10 trials from each of the four congruency 1432 

classes is selected and are used to evaluate cross decoding accuracy. 1433 

Shape Decoder Shape decoders were trained to decode the shape category of the stimulus from 1434 

RNN hidden states. Only the final 75 trials of the S1 block were eligible for inclusion in the training 1435 

set. For each decoder, we drew four trials from each of the congruency classes. These training set 1436 

were also balanced to include an even number of correct and incorrect trials. The distribution of test 1437 

trials and the methods of sampling was the same as in Color Decoder. 1438 

Response Axis Decoder Response axis decoders were trained to decode the direction of the model 1439 

response from RNN hidden states. The final 75 trials of the S1 block were eligible for inclusion in 1440 

the training set. In this case, for each decoder, we drew 16 trials which were balanced across trial 1441 

correctness and direction (4 correct trials where model responded with direction 0, 4 incorrect trials 1442 

where model responded with direction 0, 4 correct trials where model responded with direction 1, 1443 

and 4 incorrect trials where model responded with direction 1). Like above 40 test trials are drawn 1444 

from each window in the C1 blocks. In this case these trials are balanced in the same way in the 1445 

training distribution. 1446 

For each decoder we subsampled 5% of RNN hidden neurons to train decoders. Decoders were 1447 

implemented with scikit-learn’s LogisticRegression with the liblinear solver with L2 regu-1448

decoder on permuted test labels. Decoding accuracy curves plotted in the main text are cross-1450 

decoding performance averaged over all decoders for each window of trials. 1451

trends in cross decoding across different sequences of blocks. We averaged across sequence types 1453 

to obtain cross decoding on the whole dataset which we then compared to our models. 1454 

Decoder Trends Statistical Test 1455 

The cross decoding described above is primarily designed to examine whether or not there are trends 1456

each individual timestep within a trial. First, for a timestep to be considered for testing the difference 1460 

between cross decoding performance at first and last trial window had to exceed an absolute value of 1461 

0.01. At each timestep t within a trial, we computed the Sen’s slope i.e. the median of all pairwise 1462
